        Universal Studios Japan & Super Nintendo World
        What it is: Japan's biggest theme park (Osaka). Its star area, Super Nintendo World, is capacity-controlled — entering it requires timing or a ticket, and this guide is mostly about winning that game.
        Before any visit
        1

        Before any visit: buy Studio Passes online (date-locked, date-priced). Keep the QR codes available offline.

        2

        Install the official USJ app and register — area tickets are app-only. Link everyone's passes over breakfast, not at the gate.

        Pick your scenario
        • Be at the gates ~45 min before official opening — they usually open early.
        • Steady walk to the back of the park: main street → San Francisco → Jurassic Park → the green warp pipe (~10 min). No running (staff stop runners).
        • First window is free-flow: walk straight in, no ticket.
        • Stay until done — leaving means an app slot to re-enter.
        • USJ app → Area Timed Entry → Super Nintendo World → pick a slot (free).
        • Quiet weekdays: slots last into the afternoon. Weekends/holidays: often gone by 10-11.
        • No slots left? Express Passes that include a Nintendo ride carry guaranteed area entry — pricey but bulletproof.
        • The wristband (~¥4,500) makes the land playable: block punches, coins, key challenges.
        • 3 keys unlock the Bowser Jr. boss battle — played standing, no vehicle.
        • One band per person for the full experience; pairs with the app, works as an amiibo later.

        The area is walk-through-first — most of its magic needs no ride:

        • Official lists exclude back/neck/spine conditions from Mario Kart and Donkey Kong's mine cart (sudden accelerations, spins, stops).
        • Yoshi's Adventure is the gentle one — verify the posted restriction sign on the day.
        • Elsewhere: Waterworld (seated show) and the Harry Potter area as a walk-through.

        Onsen & ryokan — first-timer's guide
        What it is: a ryokan is a traditional inn — tatami rooms, futon bedding, kaiseki dinners, and (often) onsen hot-spring baths. The customs are strict-ish but simple once decoded.
        The essentials
        1

        On arrival: shoes off at the entrance (slippers provided). A yukata robe waits in your room — wearing it everywhere inside, including dinner, is correct, not lazy.

        2

        Bathe before dinner — it's the rhythm of the evening. Baths are gender-separated; swimsuits never.

        3

        Dinner is served at a set time — being late is genuinely rude to the kitchen. Confirm the hour at check-in.

        Deeper
        • Undress fully in the anteroom; bring only the SMALL towel.
        • Sit at a washing station and wash + rinse thoroughly BEFORE the tub.
        • The small towel never enters the water — fold it on your head or set it aside.
        • Soak quietly; hydrate after; no photos ever.
        • Left side over right — always (right-over-left dresses the dead).
        • Belt at the waist; the padded jacket (haori) goes on top when cold.

        Policies vary: some baths ban visible tattoos, some sell cover stickers, some offer private-bath slots. Small tattoos + cover stickers are usually fine; ask the front desk — the question is normal.

        • Futons are laid out by staff during dinner — the room transforms.
        • Step out of slippers before tatami; separate toilet slippers stay in the toilet.

        1

        Cash: 7-Bank ATMs inside 7-Eleven are the most reliable machines in Japan for foreign cards.

        Toilets in Japan
        What it is: Japanese toilets are the world's best — free, clean, everywhere — but the control panel is in Japanese and one wrong button calls staff. Two kanji solve 90% of it.
        The essentials
        1

        止 = STOP. The one button to memorize — kills spray, music, everything. Usually orange/red on the washlet panel.

        2

        Finding the flush: it is often NOT on the seat panel — look for a lever or wall panel marked 大 (big flush) / 小 (small flush), or a hand-wave sensor on the wall.

        3

        呼出 (red button) = STAFF CALL, not flush. 音姫 (Otohime) = privacy sound. If a lone red button tempts you — it summons a human.

        4

        Where to find one: every konbini (ask or walk in), every station (inside gates), every department store (cleanest — usually one per floor). You are never 5 minutes from a toilet in urban Japan.

        5

        Paper goes IN the bowl — it is designed to dissolve. The small bin is for sanitary items only.

        6

        Carry a small hand towel — many public toilets have no dryer or paper towels. Every local carries one (buy a nice one — good gift-shop excuse).

        Scenarios
        • Look for 多目的トイレ / 多機能トイレ (multi-purpose toilet) — stations, malls, museums.
        • Double the space, handrails, and a western seat — no squatting, no tight turns.
        • Marked with wheelchair signage; anyone with a physical need may use it.
        • 和式 = squat style, 洋式 = western seat. Check the door pictogram before entering.
        • In any row of stalls, at least one is usually 洋式 — often the far/accessible one.
        • Old temple/rural stations are the main squat territory; cities are nearly all western now.
        • おしり = rear spray · ビデ = bidet · 乾燥 = dryer · 水勢 = pressure · 止 = STOP.
        • Nothing sprays until you sit — the seat sensor must be pressed.
        • Worst case: stand up (spray stops), press 止, use the 大/小 lever to flush.
        • Ryokan/temples/some restaurants: dedicated slippers INSIDE the toilet room.
        • Swap into them at the toilet door, swap back out after — walking off in toilet slippers is THE classic gaijin blunder.
        Laundry in Japan
        What it is: a 37-day trip runs on coin laundry (コインランドリー) — cheap, self-service, everywhere, and most machines dispense detergent automatically.
        The essentials
        1

        Cost + time: wash ~Y300-500 (30-40 min), dryer ~Y100 per 10 min (budget 30-40 min). A full load done for ~Y700-900 in about an hour.

        2

        Finding one: Google Maps "コインランドリー" — dense in residential streets near every hotel. Many run 24h.

        3

        Detergent is usually automatic (洗剤自動投入) — if the machine lists it, bring nothing. Otherwise a vending box sells single-use packs.

        4

        Machine kanji: 洗濯 wash · 乾燥 dry · 洗濯乾燥 wash+dry in one drum · スタート start · 残り時間 time left.

        5

        Dryer heat is STRONG — Japanese gas dryers run hotter than home. Synthetics/prints: short cycles or air-dry in the room.

        6

        Etiquette: set a phone timer and come back — leaving a finished load hogging the machine is the one sin. Baskets provided are for transfers.

        Scenarios
        • Several of the trip's hotels have in-house guest washers (cheaper, no street walk — check the room binder or front desk first).
        • Evening slots fill 20:00-22:00; go at dinner time or early morning.
        • Ryokan rarely have guest machines — the village coin laundry doubles as the local social hall.
        • Start a load, walk to the baths, collect on the way back.
        • Wash-and-dry combo machine (洗濯乾燥) = drop clothes, come back in 60-90 min, pack dry.
        • Do it the night BEFORE takkyubin handovers so the shipped bag carries clean clothes.
        • In-room washers (common in apartment hotels) usually have no dryer — use the room's bathroom-dryer mode (浴室乾燥) if present, or hang overnight.
        • September humidity: thin fabrics dry overnight, jeans do not — dryer those.
        Rental car & fueling
        What it is: two car legs on this trip (Kiso/Takayama Sept 13-17, Hakone/Fuji Sept 30-Oct 2). Driving is easy — left side, polite traffic — but gas stations and toll gates have their own script.
        The essentials
        1

        The magic word: 満タン (mantan) = fill it up. At full-service stations say "regyura, mantan" (レギュラー満タン) — regular, full tank. They pump, take payment, guide you out with a bow.

        2

        Fuel type: レギュラー (regular, RED handle) is what rental cars take. 軽油 (green) is DIESEL — the kanji 軽 tricks people because kei-cars do NOT take it. When unsure: red handle.

        3

        Self-service (セルフ): touch the static-electricity pad first (静電気除去), pick レギュラー, 満タン amount, pay at the machine (cash or card). Staff exist and will help — press the call button.

        4

        ETC toll lanes: PURPLE = ETC, GREEN = 一般 (general/cash). Your ETC card (Y550 rental, from the pickup branch) goes into the dashboard reader — then use purple lanes at ~20 km/h, gate lifts itself. No card = green lane, take ticket, pay at exit.

        5

        The full-tank return rule (満タン返し): return the car with a FULL tank + keep the receipt from a station NEAR the drop-off (they may ask to see it). Skipping it = inflated per-liter charge.

        6

        Costs to expect: gas ~Y170-185/L regular · expressway tolls are serious money (Mishima→Hakone area legs are short, the Sept 13-17 leg adds up) · highway SA/PA rest stops are excellent — clean toilets, real food, regional snacks.

        Scenarios
        • Order on screen: fuel type → amount → payment. レギュラー = regular · 満タン = full · 現金 = cash · カード = card.
        • Insert nozzle fully and squeeze — it auto-stops at full.
        • レシート = receipt button. Take it (see full-tank rule).
        • Fill up within a few km of the branch, keep the receipt.
        • Arrive 10-15 min before the slot — they check the tank gauge + receipt, quick scratch walk-around, done.
        • Mishima pickup / Odawara drop-off: both are 1-2 min from their stations — plan the last fill-up before entering the station area.
        • Long descents: use engine brake — shift to B or 2/L instead of riding the brakes (fade risk).
        • Hairpins have mirrors — slow in, wide view, locals are patient.
        • Owakudani road closes nightly 17:00-9:00; mountain fog after rain is real — headlights on.
        • STOP signs (止まれ, red triangle) mean a FULL stop — police do check.
        • Rail crossings: everyone stops fully before crossing, always.
        • Turntable/lift parking in cities: hand keys to the attendant, they handle it.
        • Emergency/breakdown: call the rental company number on the key tag; JAF handles roadside.

        Restaurant customs & ticket machines
        Why this guide: Japanese restaurants run on a few systems nobody explains — the vending-machine order, the mystery appetizer charge, the name-list queue. Two minutes here saves five awkward moments.
        The systems
        • Pay FIRST at the machine by the door: cash in → press your dish → take the ticket → hand it to the counter.
        • Top-left button is usually the house specialty; some machines toggle to English.
        • Want more later? Buy another ticket.

        A small unordered dish arrives with the first drink — that's the otoshi, a ¥300-600/person seating charge. Not a mistake, not negotiable — budget it in and enjoy it.

        • Paper list at the door: write name (roman letters fine) + party size, wait for the call.
        • Tablet kiosks print numbered tickets; some show a QR to track your place remotely.
        • Standing in line without registering often does nothing — check for the list first.
        • Slurping noodles: correct and complimentary.
        • Chopsticks: never upright in rice, never passing food chopstick-to-chopstick (both are funeral rites).
        • The wet towel (oshibori) is for hands.
        • "Futari desu" — party of two.
        • "Kin'en seki onegaishimasu" — non-smoking seat please (with audio in the Phrasebook).
        • "Omakase de" — chef's choice.
